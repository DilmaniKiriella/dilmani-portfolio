About me

Beyond code, it’s about people.

I am an Information Technology undergraduate at SLIIT and currently a Junior QA Engineer at Mitra Innovation. My path into technology has not been only about writing code or using tools. It has been about understanding what people expect from software and helping teams deliver it with fewer surprises.

Working in QA has made me more patient and precise. I have learned to ask better questions, read requirements more carefully, and treat documentation as part of the product rather than an afterthought.

My long-term direction is project management. I am drawn to the space where technical work, communication, planning, and people meet.

A practical learner

Quality assurance gave me a grounded view of software delivery. It showed me how small details - a missing validation, an unclear requirement, a rushed test note - can become real issues for a team or a client.

Reflective journal

Growth that feels quieter than it looks.

The most useful professional skills I developed were not loud achievements. They were small changes in how I write, listen, speak, prepare, and respond under pressure.

01

Writing with purpose

I used to think professional writing meant sounding formal. Now I understand that it means being clear. A good email, test note, or memo should help the reader act without confusion. This became especially useful when writing defect steps and QA updates.

02

Confidence in front of people

Presentations were never my most comfortable space. Preparing structured openings, signposting key points, and closing with a clear message helped me feel less rushed. I still get nervous, but I now know how to manage that nervousness instead of letting it lead.

03

Communication inside a team

Daily standups and QA discussions taught me that communication is not about saying everything. It is about saying the right thing at the right time - progress, blockers, evidence, risk, and next action.

04

Professional maturity

My banking and QA experiences helped me understand responsibility in a more serious way. Accuracy, confidentiality, and respectful communication are not optional in professional environments. They shape how much trust others place in your work.
